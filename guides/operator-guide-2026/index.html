Pre-roll tube procurement.

This is the procurement reference we wished existed when we were the operator buying pre-roll tubes. Eight sections, ~3,000 words, one place. Every number, every spec, every state. No quote form to download it. Bookmark it.

1. The spec — what a 116mm CR pop-top actually is

The de-facto industry-standard pre-roll tube in 2026 is a 116mm-tall child-resistant pop-top, typically with a squeeze pop-top closure that detaches fully (no hinge). The dimensions are dictated by king-size pre-roll cones — 116mm tall × 21mm OD accommodates one cone up to 1.5g of flower with room for a label band.

Material is almost always polypropylene (PP). Some boutique brands use bioplastic (PLA) for sustainability positioning, but PLA tubes cost ~40% more per unit and have lower CR test pass rates. Mainstream operator choice in 2026 is PP.

The closure design is what differentiates suppliers, not the tube body. Squeeze pop-tops outperform hinged pop-tops because there's no hinge to fail over use cycles, the cap detaches fully for label inspection at retail, and they're slightly cheaper to manufacture. Pack Guys 116mm tubes use the squeeze pop-top design with molded squeeze chevrons on the cap that mark where to press.

Colors are standardized into five categories: black (opaque, premium look), clear (transparent, brand-neutral), white (opaque, label-friendly canvas), smoke (translucent, half-hide aesthetic), silver (metallic, luxury-tier finish). Custom colors and printed branding typically have higher MOQs (25K+ cases) and 8-12 week lead times.

2. Three sourcing paths — direct-China vs broker vs importer

There are exactly three ways to buy pre-roll tubes at scale in 2026. Each has a different cost, lead time, and risk profile.

Path 1 — Direct-China supplier. You contact a factory in China (typically through Alibaba or a sourcing agent), place an order, arrange ocean freight + customs clearance, and receive the goods 8-12 weeks later. Best per-unit pricing — but only at container scale (10K-25K case MOQ minimum). You absorb container risk, customs risk, currency risk, factory QA risk. If your container gets held at port, you're holding nothing for 4-6 weeks. Most operators try this once, get burned, and don't try again.

Path 2 — Domestic broker. You contact a US-based packaging distributor (Berlin Packaging, Calyx, Gamut, etc.), request a quote, get a custom price + 1-3 week lead time. The broker has already done the import work + warehoused the inventory. You pay a meaningful premium — typically 30-40% over what a direct importer would charge for the same SKU. Broker premium covers their LCL pallet freight, account-rep commission, regional warehouse overhead, and the cushion they keep for the 1-out-of-20 customer who reorders during a freight rate spike.

Path 3 — Direct importer. A supplier that imports at container scale (eliminating the broker premium) but holds inventory domestically (eliminating the 8-12 week wait). You get container-rate pricing + 1-week or faster lead time. The trade-off is product depth — direct importers typically focus on one or two spec families instead of the full packaging catalog. Pack Guys is the direct-importer path for 116mm CR tubes specifically.

3. CR certification — what it really means + how to qualify

"CR certified" is a precise regulatory term, not a marketing description. A tube is child-resistant only if its closure design has passed the federal test defined in 16 CFR § 1700.20: a panel of children ages 42-51 months attempts to open the package within a 10-minute window. To pass, no more than 20% of children can open it. A separate test verifies that 90%+ of adults can open it within a reasonable time.

The actual artifact of CR certification is a lab-issued PDF naming: the closure design, the test protocol used, the lab that ran it, the date, and the production batch tested. Not a generic certificate. Not a marketing PDF. The real PDF.

ASTM D3475 is a classification system that references the 16 CFR § 1700.20 test. When you see "ASTM D3475 + 16 CFR § 1700.20 certified" together, that's the gold standard — the closure has been classified under ASTM's framework AND tested per the federal protocol.

Red flags on supplier responses: vague certification claims, "we'll request the PDF from the factory" (no, a real supplier already has it), missing test dates, generic certificates without batch traceability. A real supplier hands the PDF over in 24 hours. Full CR cert deep-dive here.

  • 100K+ cases — case-by-case (full-container at ~1.3M units is cheapest)

The hidden cost of an opaque quote isn't only the markup — it's the calendar. A typical "request a quote" cycle eats two weeks before a single tube ships: form submission, account-rep call, NDA-flavored back-and-forth, revised quote, terms negotiation, finally a PO. Two weeks of holding flower without packaging is two weeks of revenue you're not booking.

For benchmark comparisons against your current supplier, the honest market range at 5K case is $0.07-$0.085/unit for a direct importer, $0.10-$0.13/unit for a broker quote.

5. State-by-state compliance — 20 markets covered

State compliance for pre-roll tubes is, surprisingly, mostly the same federal CR cert + a state-specific warning label. The tube itself doesn't vary; the label that goes on it does. We've published per-state compliance summaries with regulator, statute, label, and specifics:

  • California (DCC) — BPC § 26120 + universal cannabis symbol
  • Oklahoma (OMMA) — OAC 442:10-7-1 + Metrc RFID tag
  • Nevada (CCB) — NAC 678C.620 + opaque retail finish
  • New York (OCM) — NYCRR Part 124 + Surgeon General warning
  • Colorado (MED) — 1 CCR 212-3 + resealable CR
  • Michigan (CRA) — R 420.401 et seq. + MI warning symbol
  • New Jersey (CRC) — N.J.A.C. 17:30 + universal symbol
  • Massachusetts (CCC) — 935 CMR 500.105 + appeal-to-children restrictions
  • Oregon (OLCC) — OAR 845-025 + OLCC universal symbol
  • Illinois (IDFPR) — 410 ILCS 705 / Article 55
  • Arizona (ADHS) — A.A.C. R9-18 + ADHS warning copy
  • Florida (OMMU) — F.S. § 381.986 (medical-only market)

6. Payment terms — Net-30, upfront, and crypto

Net-30 is a working capital lever. The buyer holds onto cash for 30 days before paying — that's effectively a zero-interest 30-day loan from the supplier. For cannabis brands scaling fast, this is meaningful: Net-30 lets the buyer sell through product before paying for it. Full Net-30 vs upfront working-capital math here.

When to take upfront instead: when cash flow is strong AND the prompt-pay discount beats your blended cost of capital. Pack Guys offers a 2% discount on order #1 when paid via ACH within 48 hours, plus a 1.5% prompt-pay discount on USDC. If your cost of capital is below ~30% annualized, the prompt-pay discount pays.

Crypto payments are an underused lever. USDC (stablecoin) clears in seconds, costs ~0.1% in network fees, and earns the 1.5% Pack Guys prompt-pay discount. BTC and ETH also accepted. For cannabis operators who are crypto-native, USDC upfront is the cheapest path of all.

7. Qualifying a supplier — the 5 questions

The full California Buyer's Framework — adaptable to any state — is here. The five-question summary:

  1. Cert PDF. Ask for the CR cert PDF for the specific SKU. Real suppliers send it in 24 hours. Brokers stall.
  2. Lead time."Do you hold inventory in California (or your buyer's state)?" If yes, lead time is days. If no, lead time is weeks-to-months.
  3. MOQ."What's your smallest case size?" Direct-China = 10K+, broker = 500-1K, direct importer = 1K. More on MOQ.
  4. Pricing transparency."What's your posted per-unit at 1K, 5K, 25K?" If they have to "circle back," they're calling their margin manager — not their pricing system.
  5. Payment terms."When does Net-30 unlock?" Should be after a defined number of paid invoices — not a vague "contingent on credit check."

If a supplier ducks any of the five, walk. The good ones answer in plain English inside one email.
